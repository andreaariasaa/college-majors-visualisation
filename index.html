Understanding Paths to Migration

By Andrea Arias, Jimin Lee, and Keili Tucker

To be a migrant is to have your life and your story politicized. Fleeing violence or natural disasters, seeking economic opportunities, or moving for a new job in a foreign country becomes a political act rather than a personal one. Over the past decade the number of migrants from Central America to the United States has been steadily increasing and unfortunately, prejudice against migrants and fears over “caravans” of migrants have been increasing alongside. But who are the migrants? What are their stories?

All household respondents

Our data comes from a survey of around 5000 households in Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador. Many of the respondents have family members who have migrated, considered migrating, or previously attempted to migrate, but many do not. For those who did consider or previously migrated, 83.4% of respondents said their primary motivation was to find better job opportunities. Conclusions

Here we can get a sense for how lower income households fair, and see how the perceived level of violence in their areas, and understand why someone may want to migrate and why they may not be able to. We can start to understand these stories as more than dots or more than political controversy in the US. Every dot here is a household with a story and life and we hope to shed a little light on that story.
